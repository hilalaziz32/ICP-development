PAIN POINT 1: THE UGC DEATH LOOP

What the segment is saying right now:

"We spend another six to nine thousand pounds a month on UGC content... if the ad only works for three days or five days, it's such a high turnover and ads they start to not perform after a very short period of time. So we're constantly feeding the beast."
— Prospect, UK supplement brand (VoC source, sales call transcript)

"It has been our biggest failing not to have scaled paid social faster... We've tried to turn designers into campaign delivery managers and designers. We've tried to do it the other way around. We just can't seem to get it done."
— Laura Keir, CMO/CEO, Protein Works (UK supplement brand, call12 transcript)

"I have one internal designer... and she has not been able to keep up with the requests."
— Laura, GM, Jupiter (call18 transcript)

"I definitely am like in the camp of work smarter, not harder. And I feel like currently the ad library is working not smart and not hard. We have an evergreen hole."
— Kayla, Lawless Beauty (call15 transcript)

The supplement category amplifies this problem beyond what any other DTC vertical experiences. Unlike baby brands or home goods, supplement and health brands cannot freely claim outcomes — Meta's health and wellness policy prohibits disease claims, transformation promises, and implied medical outcomes. Forge Digital (March 2026) confirms this directly: "If you've tried to advertise supplements on Meta, you've probably experienced the same headache: ads get disapproved, appeals go nowhere, and your acquisition pipeline stalls." The practical consequence is that supplement brands are forced into a narrow band of structure-function language — "supports gut health," "helps maintain energy," "promotes daily wellness." Every competitor in the 5,000+ supplement brand category is running near-identical angles using the same restricted language. The creative angle pool dries up faster here than anywhere else in DTC, and what fatigues first is not the format — it's the claim. Meta's Andromeda update (globally rolled out October 2025) now rewards creative at the archetype-diversity level, not the hook-variation level. Supplement brands structurally cannot produce that diversity because compliance constraints collapse most archetype variants back into the same permitted territory.

What they're currently doing to solve it:

They are stacking a UGC retainer (Insense, Billo, Trend, Cohley — typically £6,000–£9,000/month) on top of whatever in-house design resource they have, and in many cases trying to build a "performance creative team" internally. Protein Works has three full-time content creators, a design team, a brand team, and a photography studio — and still cannot generate the volume and diversity required to scale paid social. The common workaround is to try to train designers into creative strategists who can also manage campaign delivery. Laura Keir called these people "little unicorns" and described the search as essentially fruitless.

Why it's failing:

The UGC retainer model was built for a creative half-life of 14–21 days and a brief-to-deliver cycle of the same length. That cycle was already marginal. Today's supplement UGC fatigues in 3–5 days. The content arrives already behind the algorithm, and the brand pays per asset for content that wins for less time than it took to produce. The in-house build fails for a different structural reason: even brands with full creative departments (like Protein Works, which has every capability under one roof) cannot solve the volume problem because they are trying to produce compliant assets that pass Meta's health claim review, satisfy brand guidelines, and function as direct-response performance ads simultaneously. That triple constraint means every asset requires multiple review passes before launch — adding 2–3 days to each cycle and making it impossible to reach the 200+ archetype-diverse assets per month that Meta's Andromeda engine now demands. Brands end up with a large creative team that produces slow, brand-safe content and a UGC retainer that produces fast, off-brand content, and neither stack closes the compound gap between what they can produce and what the algorithm needs to spend efficiently.

PAIN POINT 2: THE CAC CEILING NO AGENCY WILL ADMIT IS THEIR FAULT

What the segment is saying right now:

"We struggled to get Meta to be profitable at scale and we look, we want it to be, we're desperate to get there and we just found the cost of converting someone on Meta being too expensive."
— Mike Harper, Founder, Crumpit (UK functional food/gut health brand, call5 transcript)

"Going back eight months ago, we were thinking about just pulling out of America completely. We thought, you know, the Facebook costs are just far too high."
— Mike Harper, Crumpit (call5 transcript)

"Our CAC is just going up. It went up I'm thinking 10% this over the last year and it's already high, too high... with a 30 pound CAC we're obviously running too hot there."
— Prospect, UK supplement brand (VoC source, sales call transcript)

"Our ROAS is fairly low right now, about 0.6... what we need to do is get to at least 1 1/2 to 2 ROAS there to find some opportunity to be kind of break even."
— Prospect, health & wellness DTC brand (VoC source)

"CPMs are climbing, your CAC is probably 30–40% higher than it was two years ago, and your ROAS targets that made sense in 2022 are completely underwater now."
— Taylor Lagace, Co-CEO Kynship, LinkedIn post March 23, 2026 (referencing Supergut, Wildbird, and the broader DTC brand portfolio)

The supplement category is in a uniquely compressed pocket of the Meta auction. There are over 5,000 supplement brands competing for the same health-conscious consumer audience. Unlike baby products, which target identifiable life-stage triggers, or specialty foods, which have narrow demographic signals, supplement audiences are broad and heavily overlapping across all 5,000 competitors. CPM inflation in this vertical is structurally worse than the DTC average because auction density compounds platform-wide inflation. The most acute version of this pain in the transcript set — Supergut shutting off Meta entirely before working with Kynship, and Crumpit moving 90% of a $1M/month budget to AppLovin — are both health/gut health supplement brands. These are not outliers. They are the most visible expression of a ceiling that every supplement brand between $2M and $20M in revenue is experiencing in a less dramatic form right now.

What they're currently doing to solve it:

The response follows a predictable sequence. First, they rotate agencies: find a new partner who promises better results, sign a 6-month contract, get marginally different ROAS numbers, churn when the ceiling persists. Second, they migrate budget to AppLovin or TikTok Shop, treating channel substitution as an architectural fix. Crumpit moved 90% of $1M/month to AppLovin and grew from $150K to $2.5M/month — but Mike Harper identified the vulnerability himself before Kynship did: "The one metric that the app loving people don't seem to want to tell you and isn't in there is frequency." Third, they buy attribution tooling — Triple Whale, Northbeam — to get a cleaner picture of where the CAC ceiling actually sits.

Why it's failing:

Rotating agencies fails because every new agency inherits the same account architecture: daily budgets set against arbitrary ROAS targets, no P&L-anchored CAC ceiling, no financial forecast built before the first dollar is spent. The structural problem reproduces itself inside a new relationship with a new retainer. AppLovin and TikTok Shop fail because they are channel substitutions, not unit-economics fixes. The brand arrives on AppLovin with the same absent cost architecture — no P&L model, no ceiling, no frequency visibility — and the channel gives strong short-term numbers until saturation arrives invisibly. As Mike Harper observed, nobody on the AppLovin side will tell you what your frequency is. The attribution tools fail because they solve a visibility problem, not a decision problem. Brands with Triple Whale can now watch themselves cross their CAC ceiling in higher resolution. The number becomes clearer; the answer to what to do about it does not come with the dashboard. What is absent across all three remedies is the same thing: a financial model built from the brand's actual margin profile, cohort LTV, and payback window, used to set a CAC ceiling before the ad account is touched — so that every spend decision is anchored to a business outcome rather than optimized against a platform metric.

PAIN POINT 3: THE AGENCY THAT NEEDS TO BE TAUGHT

What the segment is saying right now:

"I am teaching them a little bit more than they're actually teaching us."
— Racheal, CMO, Drapeezi (call4 transcript — $28-30M DTC brand)

"I'm sick of this like performance agency kind of in a condescending way telling me that like I don't get performance ads. Like we need to be running ugly ads."
— Laura, GM, Jupiter (call18 transcript)

"I would rather just have full trust that the team is doing the right thing and that they're on top of it and kind of instead of us saying, hey, go up or down, they're coming back to us and saying, hey, we need to go up or down."
— Racheal, CMO, Drapeezi (call4 transcript)

"What we're finding is just the focus isn't there and the ability to just be agile and adjust."
— Cody, Phoenix Energy (call1 transcript)

"We've had the handbrake on whilst we've moved facilities because we've had stock out... It has been our biggest failing not to have scaled paid social faster."
— Laura Keir, CMO/CEO, Protein Works (call12 transcript)

This pain is amplified in the supplement and health category for a reason specific to this segment: the brands in this space have unusually complex P&Ls. Supplement brands typically operate with subscription attachment rates, LTV from repeat purchase cohorts, health claim compliance constraints, Amazon vendor relationships, and retail distribution — all of which feed into whether a first-order CAC is actually profitable or whether it is a loss that LTV recovers or does not. Laura Keir at Protein Works has 13 years of cohort behavior. She knows that her £29 CAC might be too conservative given her LTV — she said exactly that on the sales call. But her previous agency relationships have been built around performance partners who optimize for first-order ROAS inside the ad platform, with no visibility into the subscription economics that define Protein Works' real profitability. The result is that the CMO who understands her business at the deepest level is paying a partner to optimize a metric that doesn't reflect her actual P&L. When she pushes back, the agency condescends. When the numbers disappoint, the agency blames creative. The CMO ends up in a role inversion: she is managing the agency strategy, writing or correcting the briefs, and fact-checking the reporting — which defeats the entire purpose of the retainer.

The supplement category has a second version of this pain for founder-run brands. Founders who own both the brand vision and the P&L go in-house when agency relationships fail, believing they can solve it with the right hire. The pattern ends the same way: "It's difficult for us to really manage this without a whole lot of internal knowledge ourselves... Instead of like trying to find somebody, we let her go last week." (Jason, WOW Sports, call21 transcript). One in-house growth hire cannot cover strategy, buying, creative ops, compliance review, and analytics simultaneously — especially in a supplement category where every creative asset requires health claim review before launch. The hire exits within 90 days. The brand has spent $80K–$130K in salary and lost 6 months of compounding.

What they're currently doing to solve it:

Performance agency retainers at $8K–$25K/month, with the founder or CMO absorbing the strategic layer themselves. Some brands are adding fractional CMOs on top to provide the strategic oversight the agency lacks. A growing number are firing agencies and going in-house, hiring a Head of Growth or Director of Acquisition at $90K–$130K base to run paid internally. Protein Works tried all three variations — external agency, in-house team build, hybrid — and described the entire journey as "our biggest failing."

Why it's failing:

Performance agencies are structured to report on what happened inside the ad account, not to build the financial architecture that determines what should happen. They wait for direction on creative and CAC targets rather than engineering those targets from the P&L themselves. When the supplement CMO provides direction based on her cohort data and subscription economics, the agency interprets it as client interference rather than essential business context — and tells her to "run ugly ads" rather than engaging with why her unit economics require a specific approach. Fractional CMOs solve the strategic gap but leave the implementation gap open: they diagnose the problem correctly and hand a brief to a media buyer who still doesn't know the brand's payback window. The in-house build fails because the four functions required for performance marketing at scale in a supplement brand — financial strategy, media buying, creative production with compliance review, and analytics — cannot be covered by one person. They overlap in ways that require a coordinated team, not a single hire who is expected to be simultaneously a CFO-adjacent strategist, a hands-on buyer, a creative director, and a data analyst.
